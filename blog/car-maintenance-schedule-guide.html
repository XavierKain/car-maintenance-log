Your car is probably the second most expensive thing you own. Yet most people have no idea when their next oil change is due, when their brake pads were last replaced, or whether their timing belt is about to snap. A clear maintenance schedule changes everything — it keeps your car running longer, prevents expensive breakdowns, and preserves resale value.

This guide breaks down every maintenance task your vehicle needs, organized by mileage intervals. Whether you drive a brand-new sedan or a 15-year-old truck, these intervals apply to most vehicles. Always cross-reference with your owner's manual for manufacturer-specific recommendations.

Why Maintenance Schedules Matter

Skipping scheduled maintenance doesn't save money — it costs more in the long run. A missed oil change can lead to engine sludge buildup, which causes premature wear on internal components. A forgotten coolant flush can lead to overheating. Ignored brake pads destroy rotors, turning a $150 job into a $600 one.

According to AAA, the average cost of vehicle maintenance and repair is about $0.09 per mile. For a car driven 15,000 miles per year, that's $1,350 annually. But cars that follow their maintenance schedule consistently have significantly lower repair costs over their lifetime compared to neglected vehicles.

Beyond cost, there's safety. Worn tires lose traction. Old brake fluid absorbs moisture and reduces braking performance. A cracked serpentine belt can leave you stranded on the highway. Regular maintenance isn't just about keeping your car happy — it's about keeping you safe.

There's also the resale angle. A car with a documented maintenance history sells for 10-15% more than one without records. Buyers trust vehicles that have been cared for. Every 5,000 Miles (or 6 Months)

This is your baseline interval — the stuff you should do twice a year or every 5,000 miles, whichever comes first. These are quick, inexpensive services that prevent the most common problems.

Oil and Filter Change

Engine oil lubricates, cools, and cleans internal engine components. Over time, it breaks down, loses viscosity, and accumulates contaminants. Most modern cars using synthetic oil can go 5,000-7,500 miles between changes, though some manufacturers now recommend up to 10,000 miles with full synthetic.

Always change the oil filter at the same time. A clogged filter restricts oil flow and defeats the purpose of fresh oil. Cost: $30-75 for conventional, $65-125 for full synthetic at a shop. DIY cost: $25-45 for oil and filter.

Tire Rotation

Front tires wear differently than rear tires due to weight distribution and steering forces. Rotating them every 5,000-7,500 miles ensures even wear across all four tires, which extends their lifespan by up to 20%. Most tire shops do this for $20-50, and many include it free with tire purchases.

Multi-Point Inspection

Every oil change should include a visual inspection of key components: tire pressure and tread depth, fluid levels (coolant, brake, power steering, washer), belt and hose condition, battery terminals, and lights. Most shops include this with an oil change service. If you're doing your own oil changes, take 10 minutes to check these items yourself.

Windshield Wipers

Wiper blades degrade from UV exposure, temperature changes, and general use. Most last 6-12 months before they start streaking. At $15-30 per set, there's no reason to drive with worn blades that compromise visibility in rain.

Every 15,000 Miles (or 12 Months)

At this interval, you're adding services that address components with moderate wear cycles. These are still relatively affordable but often overlooked.

Air Filter Replacement

The engine air filter prevents dust, pollen, and debris from entering the combustion chamber. A clogged filter restricts airflow, reducing fuel efficiency by up to 10% and potentially causing rough idling. Replacement is simple — often a 5-minute DIY job — and filters cost $15-30.

Cabin Air Filter

This filter cleans the air coming through your HVAC system. It traps pollen, dust, mold spores, and pollutants. If your AC smells musty or airflow seems weak, a clogged cabin filter is often the culprit. Cost: $15-25 for the filter, usually easy to replace yourself. Location varies — check your owner's manual.

Brake Inspection

While brakes should be visually checked at every oil change, a thorough inspection at 15,000 miles means measuring brake pad thickness, checking rotor condition, and inspecting brake lines. Brake pads generally last 30,000-70,000 miles depending on driving style, but catching thin pads early prevents rotor damage.

Battery Test

Car batteries last 3-5 years on average, but heat, cold, and short trips accelerate degradation. A load test at 15,000-mile intervals catches weak batteries before they leave you stranded. Most auto parts stores offer free battery testing.

Every 30,000 Miles (or 2 Years)

The 30,000-mile service is where things get more comprehensive. Several important fluids and components reach their replacement interval here.

Transmission Fluid

Automatic transmission fluid lubricates gears, cools the transmission, and provides hydraulic pressure for gear changes. Over time, it breaks down and can cause rough shifting, slipping, or transmission failure. Many manufacturers recommend changing it every 30,000-60,000 miles. Cost: $150-300 for a drain and fill, $250-500 for a full flush.

Coolant Flush

Engine coolant (antifreeze) prevents overheating in summer and freezing in winter. It also contains corrosion inhibitors that protect internal engine components. Over time, these additives deplete. A coolant flush replaces all the old fluid with fresh coolant. Cost: $100-200 at a shop.

Power Steering Fluid

If your car has hydraulic power steering (not electric), the fluid should be inspected and potentially replaced at this interval. Dark, burnt-smelling fluid needs replacement. Cost: $80-150 for a flush.

Fuel Filter

The fuel filter catches contaminants before they reach the engine. On older vehicles with serviceable fuel filters, replacement every 30,000 miles prevents fuel delivery issues. Many newer cars have lifetime filters integrated into the fuel pump assembly. Check your owner's manual.

Spark Plugs (Copper)

If your vehicle uses copper spark plugs, they need replacement around 30,000 miles. Signs of worn plugs include rough idle, poor acceleration, and decreased fuel economy. Iridium and platinum plugs last much longer — see the 60,000-mile section. Cost: $40-150 depending on engine configuration.

Every 60,000 Miles (or 4 Years)

At 60,000 miles, you're entering the territory where larger, more expensive services come due. These are the services that, if skipped, can lead to catastrophic (and costly) failures.

Brake Fluid Flush

Brake fluid is hygroscopic — it absorbs moisture from the air over time. Water in brake fluid lowers its boiling point, which can cause brake fade under heavy braking (like descending a mountain). A flush every 2-3 years or 60,000 miles ensures optimal braking performance. Cost: $100-200.

Spark Plugs (Iridium/Platinum)

Modern iridium or platinum spark plugs last 60,000-100,000 miles. When they wear, the electrode gap widens, causing misfires, poor fuel economy, and rough running. Replacing them on schedule prevents catalytic converter damage from unburned fuel. Cost: $100-300 depending on the number of cylinders and accessibility.

Suspension Inspection

Shocks and struts typically last 50,000-100,000 miles. At 60,000 miles, have a mechanic inspect them along with ball joints, tie rod ends, and bushings. Worn suspension components affect handling, braking distance, and tire wear. Replacement cost: $500-1,500 for a set of shocks or struts installed.

Drive Belt Replacement

The serpentine belt drives your alternator, AC compressor, power steering pump, and sometimes the water pump. Modern belts last 60,000-100,000 miles, but a failure leaves you stranded immediately. Inspect for cracks, fraying, or glazing. Cost: $100-250 to replace.

Every 100,000 Miles

The 100,000-mile mark is a major milestone. Several critical components reach end of life here, and addressing them proactively can mean the difference between a car that lasts 200,000 miles and one that doesn't make it to 120,000.

Timing Belt Replacement

If your engine uses a timing belt (not a timing chain), this is critical. A broken timing belt on an interference engine causes pistons to collide with valves — destroying the engine. Replacement every 60,000-100,000 miles is essential. Cost: $500-1,000, often done with the water pump since it's accessed during the same job.

Water Pump

The water pump circulates coolant through the engine. It's often replaced alongside the timing belt since they share the same access point. Failure causes rapid overheating and potential engine damage. Cost when done with timing belt: $100-200 additional.

Major Fluid Service

At 100,000 miles, every fluid in the vehicle should be inspected and likely replaced: transmission fluid, differential fluid (if equipped), transfer case fluid (AWD/4WD), and coolant. Consider this a comprehensive reset of all fluids.

Ignition Coils

Ignition coils can start failing around 100,000 miles. Symptoms include misfires, check engine light, and rough running. While they don't all need replacement at once, it's cost-effective to replace them as a set during spark plug service. Cost: $200-500 for a full set.

Seasonal Maintenance

Some maintenance tasks aren't mileage-based — they follow the calendar.

Before Winter

  • Test battery capacity (cold weather reduces battery output by up to 50%)
  • Check coolant concentration with a hydrometer (should be 50/50 mix)
  • Inspect tire tread depth — consider winter tires if you live in a snowy region
  • Test heater and defroster operation
  • Switch to winter-rated windshield washer fluid
  • Check that all lights are functioning (shorter days mean more night driving)

Before Summer

  • Test AC system — recharge refrigerant if cooling is weak
  • Check coolant level and condition (overheating is the #1 summer breakdown cause)
  • Inspect tires for proper inflation (heat increases tire pressure)
  • Replace wiper blades if worn (summer storms demand good visibility)
  • Check belts and hoses (heat accelerates rubber degradation)

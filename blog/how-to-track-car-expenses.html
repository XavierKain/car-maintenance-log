Most car owners know roughly what they pay for gas each month. Some remember the last oil change. Almost nobody knows their actual total cost of ownership. According to AAA's 2024 driving costs study, the average American spends $12,182 per year on vehicle ownership — that's over $1,000 per month. Yet when you ask people what their car costs them, most guess less than half that figure.

The disconnect comes from invisible costs. You feel the sting of filling up the tank, but you don't notice the $5 per day your car depreciates just sitting in the driveway. You remember the $800 brake job, but forget the $200 in registration fees you paid six months ago. Tracking these expenses isn't just for budgeting nerds — it's for anyone who wants to make smarter decisions about the machines they depend on every day.

The True Cost of Owning a Car

Before we get into tracking methods, let's establish what "car expenses" actually includes. Most people only track direct, frequent costs like fuel and maintenance. But the total cost of ownership encompasses much more:

Notice that depreciation — the loss in your car's value over time — is the single largest expense, yet almost nobody tracks it. Fuel, which feels like the biggest expense because you pay for it weekly, is actually only about a fifth of the total.

Understanding this breakdown helps you make better decisions. Should you buy a new car that depreciates fast but needs less maintenance, or a used one that's already depreciated but might need more repairs? The answer depends on your specific numbers — and you can only know your numbers if you track them.

Expense Categories to Track

To get a clear picture, organize your expenses into these categories:

Fixed Costs (Monthly/Annual)

  • Car payment or lease: Your monthly financing cost
  • Insurance: Comprehensive, collision, liability premiums
  • Registration: Annual state registration and inspection fees
  • Taxes: Property tax on vehicles (in applicable states)

Variable Costs (As Incurred)

  • Fuel: Gas or electricity for EVs
  • Maintenance: Scheduled services (oil changes, tire rotations, fluid flushes)
  • Repairs: Unscheduled fixes (broken parts, accident damage)
  • Tires: New tires, alignments, balancing
  • Parking: Garage fees, meters, event parking
  • Tolls: Highway and bridge tolls
  • Car washes: Exterior and interior cleaning

Hidden Costs

  • Depreciation: The difference between what you paid and what it's worth now
  • Opportunity cost: What your money could earn if invested instead
  • Time cost: Hours spent on maintenance, fueling, dealing with breakdowns

You don't need to track every single sub-category from day one. Start with fuel and maintenance — the two most actionable categories — then expand over time.

Tracking Methods Compared

There are several ways to track car expenses, each with trade-offs:

Paper Notebook

The old-school approach. Keep a small notebook in your glove box and write down every expense. Pros: no technology needed, always accessible. Cons: easy to lose, hard to search or calculate totals, no backup, not easy to share when selling.

Spreadsheet

Google Sheets or Excel gives you formulas, charts, and sorting. Create columns for date, mileage, category, description, and amount. Pros: flexible, powerful calculations, free. Cons: clunky on mobile, requires manual data entry with no reminders, easy to abandon.

Banking App Categories

Some banking apps let you tag transactions by category. Pros: automatic capture of card purchases. Cons: misses cash payments, doesn't capture mileage, no maintenance-specific features like reminders or photo receipts.

Dedicated Car Maintenance App

Purpose-built apps like Car Maintenance Log are designed specifically for vehicle expense tracking. They combine service logging, cost tracking, photo receipts, and reminders in one interface. Pros: designed for the task, includes reminders, photo receipts, per-vehicle tracking, exportable data. Cons: requires downloading an app (though PWAs work in the browser).

For most people, a dedicated app offers the best balance of convenience and functionality. The key is choosing one you'll actually use consistently.

Tracking Fuel Costs

Fuel is the most frequent car expense, which makes it both the easiest to track and the easiest to ignore (since each fill-up seems small). Here's what to record each time you fuel up:

  • Date
  • Odometer reading
  • Gallons/liters filled
  • Total cost
  • Price per gallon/liter
  • Full tank or partial fill

With this data, you can calculate your actual miles per gallon (MPG) by dividing miles driven since last fill-up by gallons added. This is more accurate than your car's trip computer, which often reads 5-10% optimistically.

Tracking MPG over time also serves as a diagnostic tool. A sudden drop in fuel economy can indicate mechanical issues — underinflated tires, a clogged air filter, failing oxygen sensor, or dragging brakes. If your MPG drops 15% or more without a change in driving habits, something's wrong.

Pro tip: Always fill to full and track miles between fill-ups. Partial fills make MPG calculations inaccurate.

For electric vehicle owners, track kWh consumed per charge and cost per kWh. Home charging rates differ from public charger rates, so note the source. Calculate your cost per mile and compare it to gas equivalents — most EV owners spend 3-5 cents per mile on electricity vs. 10-15 cents for gas.

Tracking Maintenance Costs

Maintenance and repair costs are the most important category to track because they're the most actionable. Knowing you spent $2,400 on repairs last year tells you something. Knowing that $1,800 of that went to a single recurring problem tells you much more.

For every maintenance event, record:

  • Date and mileage
  • Service type (oil change, brakes, tires, etc.)
  • Parts cost vs. labor cost (helps compare shop vs. DIY)
  • Shop name (track which shops give the best value)
  • Photo of receipt/invoice (proof for warranty claims and resale)
  • Notes (what was recommended for next time)

Over time, this data reveals patterns. You might discover that a particular shop charges 30% more for the same service. Or that your car's transmission issues started creeping in at 80,000 miles. Or that you spend more on one vehicle than another, helping you decide which to keep and which to sell.

With an app like Car Maintenance Log, you can snap a photo of the receipt right at the shop counter, log the service with date and mileage, and set a reminder for the next interval — all in under a minute. The receipt photo is particularly valuable: paper receipts fade within a year, but a digital photo lasts forever.

Insurance and Registration

Insurance is a major expense that many people set and forget. But tracking it year over year reveals important trends and opportunities:

  • Annual premium trend: Is your rate increasing each year? By how much?
  • Per-vehicle cost: If you have multiple vehicles, which ones cost more to insure?
  • Deductible analysis: Would raising your deductible from $500 to $1,000 save enough to be worthwhile?
  • Shopping savings: How much did you save (or could you save) by comparing quotes?

Record your insurance premium, deductible amounts, coverage levels, and renewal date. Set a reminder 30 days before renewal to shop for competing quotes. Studies show that drivers who compare quotes annually save an average of $400 per year.

Registration fees are simpler but still worth tracking, especially if you own multiple vehicles or live in a state with emissions testing requirements. Note the renewal date, amount paid, and any inspection costs.

Understanding Depreciation

Depreciation is the silent killer of car budgets. A new car loses roughly 20% of its value in the first year and about 15% per year for the next four years. By year five, your $35,000 car might be worth $14,000 — that's $21,000 lost, or $4,200 per year.

While you can't stop depreciation, tracking it helps you:

  • Time your purchase: Buying a 2-3 year old car lets someone else absorb the steepest depreciation
  • Time your sale: Selling before major depreciation events (warranty expiration, high mileage milestones) preserves value
  • Compare true costs: A "cheap" car that depreciates fast may cost more than an "expensive" one that holds its value

To track depreciation, note your purchase price and check your car's current market value every 6-12 months using Kelley Blue Book, Edmunds, or similar tools. The difference is your depreciation cost for that period.

Calculating Your Cost Per Mile

The ultimate metric for car expense tracking is cost per mile (CPM). It normalizes all expenses into a single, comparable number. Here's the formula:

Cost Per Mile = Total Annual Expenses ÷ Annual Miles Driven

Include everything: fuel, insurance, maintenance, repairs, depreciation, registration, financing, parking, and tolls. AAA's 2024 average is $0.82 per mile for a new car. Older, paid-off vehicles typically run $0.30-0.50 per mile.

Cost per mile is especially powerful when comparing vehicles. If your SUV costs $0.75/mile and your sedan costs $0.40/mile, you know exactly how much each trip costs in each vehicle. It also helps with the buy-vs-repair decision: if repair costs push your CPM above $0.60-0.70/mile, it might be more economical to replace the vehicle.

Track your CPM quarterly. It fluctuates with large repairs and seasonal fuel costs, but the annual average is your key number. If it's trending up year over year, your car is getting more expensive to own — a signal to start planning your next move.

Tips for Consistent Tracking

The best tracking system is the one you actually use. Here's how to build the habit:

1. Track Immediately

Log expenses at the point of purchase, not later. Sitting at the gas pump? Log it while the tank fills. Just paid the mechanic? Log it in the parking lot. Delayed logging leads to forgotten entries. With Car Maintenance Log, you can add an entry and snap a receipt photo in under 30 seconds.

2. Set Recurring Reminders

Some expenses are predictable: insurance renewal, registration, scheduled maintenance. Set calendar reminders or use an app with built-in reminder features so these never surprise you.

3. Review Monthly

Spend 5 minutes at the end of each month reviewing your expenses. Are you spending more than expected? Is a particular category growing? Monthly reviews catch issues early.

4. Keep Receipts Digitally

Paper receipts fade, get lost, and clutter your glove box. Take a photo of every receipt immediately and attach it to the expense entry. This creates a permanent, searchable record that proves maintenance history when selling.

5. Track All Vehicles Together

If you own multiple vehicles, track them in the same system so you can compare costs side by side. You might be surprised which vehicle actually costs more to own. Apps that support multiple vehicle profiles make this easy.

6. Export Annually

At the end of each year, export your data as a CSV or PDF. Create an annual summary with totals by category. This becomes your financial record and maintenance documentation — invaluable for tax purposes (if you use your vehicle for business) and for resale value.
